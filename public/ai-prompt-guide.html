The basics

What is prompting?

A prompt is simply a clear, written brief. You describe what you need in plain English — who it's for, what it should do, and how it should feel — and Claude turns that brief into a working UI design. The clearer the brief, the better the result. You're not coding; you're describing intent well.

1 Image references

Show, don't tell.

A screenshot carries layout, density and tone in an instant — context that would take paragraphs to describe, and that Claude reads straight from the pixels. Attach 2–3 before you write a word.

Reuse our own work
Screenshots from past Applab projects already carry our design language — Claude picks it up automatically.
Two references beat one
Claude cross-references them for shared patterns: sidebar conventions, card anatomy, chart density.
Words vs. images
"Make it modern and clean" — means something different to everyone.
Attach 2–3 references — even an Awwwards or Dribbble shot — then write around them.
In your promptMatch the structure & density of these two references.
Key takeaway

Two good screenshots can write 80% of the prompt for you.

2 Branding file

Hand over the brand.

Brand is the fastest thing to get wrong and the slowest to describe. Give Claude the real source and it extracts the colours, fonts and identity rules itself — no guessing, no drift.

Feed it the source
A brand guideline PDF, a logo (SVG/PNG) for accurate colour sampling, or the client's live URL.
Always attach ours
For any Applab work, include the Applab brand file so the output is on-system from the first draft.
Guessed vs. read
Hard-coding hex values you guessed — they drift from the client's real palette.
Let Claude read the live brand and pull the exact palette, fonts and logo spacing.
In your promptUse the brand at gba.gco.gov.qa — exact colours, Inter + Noto Arabic.
Key takeaway

The brand should come from a file, not a description.

3 Be specific

Name everything.

Vagueness is the number-one cause of generic output. Every detail you leave out, Claude fills with a safe, average guess — so specify the things you actually care about.

Every screen state
Empty, loading, error and success — not just the happy path everyone remembers.
Motion & numbers
Durations, easings, font sizes and exact dimensions — not "nice animations".
Vague vs. specific
"Stunning UI with animations and a great look and feel."
"Staggered fade-up 100ms; counters 0→final; charts draw in on scroll."
Key takeaway

If you can measure it, name it in the prompt.

4 Give Claude a role

Cast the designer.

Claude calibrates its quality bar to the role you assign. A "helpful assistant" and a "senior creative director" produce very different work from the very same brief.

Seniority + domain
"Senior UI Designer specialising in government enterprise dashboards" beats "a designer".
Match role to goal
Need motion? Cast a motion designer. Need data density? An enterprise-dashboard specialist.
Default vs. cast
Skip the role and accept whatever default voice you happen to get.
Open with a specific, ambitious persona before the brief begins.
In your promptYou are a Senior Creative Director + Motion Designer shipping Awwwards SOTD winners.
Key takeaway

The more specific the role, the higher the bar Claude sets for itself.

5 Design taste

Have a point of view.

Claude can hit almost any aesthetic — but only if you point at one. "Make it look good" has no target; a named benchmark, a feeling and an audience give it something to reach for.

Set a benchmark
Name Linear, Stripe, an Awwwards SOTD winner, or a competitor the client admires.
Name feeling + audience
"Authoritative, deliberate, no decorative excess" — and "must impress the CEO of MCIT".
Undefined vs. defined
"Make it modern and premium" — you get the average of everything.
"Editorial and immersive like Linear; calm and authoritative for a ministry."
Key takeaway

You can't reach "great" until you've defined it.

6 Iterate ×3

The first output is a start.

Treating round one as the finished product is the most common reason teams give up on AI design. The polish almost always lives in rounds two and three.

Build, don't restart
Each follow-up refines the last output. Starting fresh throws away everything Claude has learned.
Feedback that lands
"Make it better" does nothing. "Bold the KPIs, sidebar to 52px, add chart draw-in" is a leap.
1Structure → 2Brand + motion → 3Polish
Key takeaway

Three focused rounds beat one "perfect" attempt every time.

7 No AI slop

Set the fundamentals.

A handful of specifics in the prompt are the difference between "designed" and "obviously generated". Name them and the tell-tale AI signatures simply disappear.

Real charts & icons
ECharts or Highcharts for data; Lucide or Phosphor for icons — never emojis.
Real type, calm depth
Specify Inter, Geist or a serif; strip decorative shadows and keep elevation subtle.
Slop vs. designed
Emoji icons, rainbow gradients, heavy drop-shadows, fake chart images.
A real charts library, one accent, one type system, restrained elevation.
In your promptCharts: ECharts. Icons: Lucide. Font: Inter. Shadows: subtle only.
Key takeaway

Name the fundamentals and the "AI look" disappears.

Example 01 · Healthcare

Patient appointment booking

Objective. Help patients book a doctor's appointment in under a minute, with zero confusion and a calm, trustworthy feel.

Why the after works
It names the user, the goal, the emotion, and concrete constraints — so Claude designs for a real patient, not a generic form.
Expected outcome
A two-step flow: choose a specialist, then a time slot — with large tap targets, clear progress, and a reassuring confirmation screen.
healthcare-booking.prompt
A vague ask vs. a real brief
# The vague ask Make a booking screen for a hospital app. Make it look nice and easy to use. # What Claude is left to guess - Who books — patients, or front-desk staff? - "Easy" by whose standard? Older users? In a hurry? - One screen, or the whole flow? - Any accessibility, brand, or platform needs?
# Role You are a Senior UI/UX Designer specialising in calm, accessible healthcare apps. # Context Design a patient appointment booking screen for a hospital app. # Audience Patients of all ages, including first-time and older users. Many are stressed or unwell while booking. # Be specific - A clear two-step flow: pick a specialist, then a time slot - One obvious primary action per screen; visible progress; easy back - A reassuring confirmation screen at the end - Touch targets ≥ 44px, readable contrast, labels on every field # Taste Calm, clean, clinical — trustworthy, not flashy. Reference quality: Apple Health meets Stripe. # Deliverables Responsive desktop + mobile. Give me two variations, then we iterate.
Example 02 · Fintech

Investment dashboard

Objective. Give active investors a clear, confident view of their portfolio performance at a single glance.

Why the after works
It calls out the data hierarchy and the emotional bar — "confident, not overwhelming" — which is exactly what data-heavy screens get wrong.
Expected outcome
A dashboard with one headline number, a clean performance chart, and scannable holdings — using colour and weight, not clutter, to guide the eye.
fintech-dashboard.prompt
A vague ask vs. a real brief
# The vague ask Build me an investment dashboard. Show the portfolio and make it look professional. # What Claude is left to guess - Which numbers matter most, and in what order? - How data-savvy is the user? How much time? - "Professional" like what — a bank, or a startup? - Light, dark, mobile? Any accessibility bar?
# Role You are a Senior Product Designer for premium fintech, fluent in dense data. # Context Design an investment dashboard home screen for a fintech app. # Audience Active retail investors who check daily — data-comfortable, time-poor. # Be specific — data hierarchy 1. One headline figure: total value + today's change 2. A clean performance chart (ECharts) with range filters 3. A scannable list of top holdings 4. Secondary stats below the fold # Constraints & taste - WCAG AA; never colour alone — pair gains/losses with an icon - Tabular numbers; guide the eye with weight, not clutter - Confident and premium. Reference: Linear meets a private bank.

Common mistakes

Poor prompt vs. better prompt

The same request, two ways. Notice how little extra effort the better version takes.

Poor prompt
Vague — Claude has to guess
"Make me a nice dashboard."
No audience — who is it for?
No goal — what should it achieve?
"Nice" means nothing measurable.
No references, no role, no constraints.
Better prompt
Specific — Claude has what it needs
"You are a senior dashboard designer. [2 refs + brand] A sales dashboard for regional managers: this month's revenue first, a trend chart, top 5 deals. Confident and clean, like Linear. ECharts, Lucide icons, WCAG AA."
Sets a role and attaches references.
Sets the data hierarchy: revenue, then trend, then deals.
Gives a quality bar: "clean, like Linear."
Names the UI basics: real charts, real icons, AA.
